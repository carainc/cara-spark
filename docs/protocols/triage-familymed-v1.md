## Family Medicine Triage: Structured Assessment

> **Scope.** This protocol covers **adolescents, adults, and older adults**, plus **pregnancy and the postpartum period**. For infants and young children, use the **pediatric triage protocol** (the gates below assume an adolescent/adult body and physiology). In a true full-spectrum family practice you may run both side by side — when a young child is the patient, switch to the pediatric gates.
>
> **Caller frame.** The patient may be calling **for themselves** or a **caregiver may be calling on their behalf**. Adapt the phrasing accordingly: ask the patient directly when you can, and confirm observable details with whoever can see the patient right now.
>
> **What this is.** Decision-support scaffolding modeled on the Schmitt-Thompson adult telephone protocols (highest-acuity questions first, disposition-driven). It standardizes the floor, not the ceiling — clinical judgment still rules.

### Three-Phase Pattern

**Phase 1: Understand (Collaborative opening, broad to narrow questioning)**
- Warm Reflective Anchor: Validate the specific symptom or situation named (e.g., "Chest pain that came on while you were resting — that's worth taking seriously," not "I understand"). Prove you listened.
- The Pivot: Move from validation to assessment without lingering. "What worries you most about how you're feeling right now?" (or, for a caregiver, "about how they look right now?")
- Wayfinding and collaboration statement using "Let's," calibrated to severity:
  - "Let's get a clear picture so we can pick the safest next step."
  - "Let's make sure nothing dangerous is going on first."
- What's different or unusual compared to their baseline or prior episodes ("Is this like anything you've had before, or new?").
- Consider pacing cues if interactions extend more than ~3 turns; if the patient sounds breathless or is struggling to speak, shorten everything and escalate your suspicion.

### Phase 2: Assess Red Flags (Teaching as you go)

- Confirm subjective terms with observable, answerable detail:
  - "Chest pain" → "Tell me what it feels like — pressure, squeezing, sharp? Does it spread to your arm, jaw, neck, or back? Are you sweaty, nauseated, or short of breath with it? Did it start with exertion?"
  - "Trouble breathing" → "Can you speak a full sentence without stopping for air? Is this at rest or only with activity? Are your lips or fingertips bluish?"
  - "Dizzy" → "Is the room spinning, or do you feel like you might pass out, or just weak? Is it worse when you stand up?"
  - "Weak" / "numb" → "Is it your whole body, or one side? Can you raise both arms equally and smile evenly? Is your speech clear?"
  - "Confused" → "Is this new today? Do they know where they are and roughly the time? Are they making sense and recognizing people?"
  - "Worst headache of my life" → "Did it hit maximum intensity within seconds, like a thunderclap, or build over time? Any fever, stiff neck, vision change, or weakness?"
  - "A lot of bleeding" → "Where from? What color — bright red, dark, coffee-ground vomit, black tarry stool? Is it still going? Are you on any blood thinners?"
- Ask **one question at a time** if the patient is terse, breathless, frightened, or in pain.
- Explain your thinking when it helps:
  - "I'm asking whether it spreads to your jaw or arm because that pattern points toward the heart."
  - "I'm asking about new confusion because in an infection that can be the first sign things are getting serious."
- Then move through Tier 1 (Core Gates) and Tier 2 (Conditional Gates).

**Phase 3: Sentinel Check (Before disposition)**
Before any final recommendation, ask yourself: What are 2–3 dangerous conditions this could be? Do I have the data to reasonably rule them out? If not, ask NOW before deciding the triage level.

### Critical Red Flags — Tier 1 Core Gates (Always Assess If Potentially Relevant)

### Chest pain / possible heart attack (ACS) — and dangerous chest-pain mimics
- **Confirm**
  - Character (pressure, tightness, squeezing, heaviness) and location (often central/substernal).
  - Radiation to arm(s), jaw, neck, shoulder, back, or upper abdomen ("indigestion").
  - Associated diaphoresis (cold sweat), nausea/vomiting, shortness of breath, lightheadedness, sense of doom.
  - Trigger and duration: exertional? lasting more than a few minutes? not clearly reproduced by pressing on the chest or by position/breathing?
  - **Atypical presentations** are common and dangerous in **women, older adults, and people with diabetes**: shortness of breath alone, unusual fatigue, jaw/back pain, epigastric "heartburn," or near-fainting — sometimes without classic chest pain.
  - **Dissection flag:** sudden "tearing" or "ripping" pain, maximal at onset, radiating to the back, or a big difference in arms.
  - **PE flag:** sharp pain worse with a deep breath, plus breathlessness and fast heart rate, especially with clot risk factors (recent surgery/immobility, cancer, estrogen, prior clot).
- **Disposition**
  - Any chest pain with ACS features, or new/unexplained chest pressure with sweating/SOB/nausea, or dissection/PE features → **er_911**. Do not drive yourself. If not allergic and no bleeding/contraindication and it's available, chew one adult aspirin (325 mg) while waiting, per local guidance.
  - Clearly non-cardiac, reproducible-on-palpation, brief, well-appearing, low-risk patient → **needs_review** (still verify no red flags before downgrading).

### Stroke signs (BE-FAST) — including symptoms that already resolved (TIA)
- **Confirm (sudden onset of any of these)**
  - **B**alance: sudden loss of balance or coordination.
  - **E**yes: sudden vision loss or double vision, one or both eyes.
  - **F**ace: drooping or asymmetry — "Can you smile? Is one side lower?"
  - **A**rm (or leg): weakness or numbness, often one-sided — "Can you hold both arms up? Does one drift?"
  - **S**peech: slurred, or trouble producing or understanding words.
  - **T**ime: note the **time the patient was last known well** — this drives treatment options.
  - Also flag sudden severe headache or sudden confusion.
- **Disposition**
  - Any acute focal deficit → **er_911** now. Do not "wait to see if it passes."
  - **Symptoms that have fully resolved (likely TIA) are still an emergency** → er_911 / immediate ER; short-term stroke risk is high.

### Severe difficulty breathing
- **Confirm**
  - Can they speak in full sentences, or only a few words between breaths?
  - At rest vs only on exertion; new vs chronic (known COPD/asthma/CHF) and how it compares to baseline.
  - Color (blue/gray lips or fingertips), audible wheeze or stridor, gasping.
  - Oxygen saturation if a reliable device is available.
  - Associated chest pain, leg swelling, fever, or known heart/lung disease.
- **Disposition**
  - Severe (can't speak more than a few words, blue/gray, gasping, O₂ <90% with symptoms, or exhaustion) → **er_911**.
  - Moderate (clearly increased work of breathing, faster than usual, struggling but maintaining) → **er_drive**.
  - Mild and stable, talking comfortably, known cause responding to usual rescue treatment → **needs_review**.

### Anaphylaxis / severe allergic reaction
- **Confirm**
  - Swelling of lips, tongue, throat, or face.
  - Trouble breathing or swallowing, tight throat, voice change.
  - Widespread hives, vomiting, or dizziness/faintness after an exposure (food, sting, drug).
  - Epinephrine available (EpiPen/Auvi-Q)? If they have a history of anaphylaxis, advise using it **early** when symptoms are moderate or worsening — don't wait.
- **Disposition**
  - Any trouble breathing/swallowing, throat tightness, faintness, OR epinephrine used → **er_911** (epinephrine then 911).
  - New facial/lip swelling or hives with vomiting/dizziness but breathing clearly OK → **er_drive**.

### Possible sepsis / serious infection
- **Confirm** (a known or suspected infection — UTI, pneumonia, skin/wound, flu, post-procedure — **plus** any of these)
  - New confusion or slurred speech.
  - Extreme shivering/rigors or severe muscle pain.
  - Passing little or no urine all day.
  - Severe breathlessness.
  - "I feel like I'm going to die" / overwhelming sense of being very unwell.
  - Skin mottled, blotchy, very pale, or clammy/sweaty; very fast heart rate or breathing.
  - Temperature very high **or** abnormally low (either extreme matters).
- **Older adults & immunocompromised:** new confusion or "just not right" may be the **only** sign, and they may be **afebrile**. Lower your threshold.
- **Disposition**
  - Infection + any sepsis warning sign → **er_drive** (er_911 if poor perfusion, hard to rouse, or breathing trouble). Advise the patient/family to tell the ER "I'm concerned about sepsis."

### New confusion / altered mental status (delirium)
- **Confirm**
  - New vs baseline (ask someone who knows them).
  - Oriented to person/place/time? Making sense? Recognizing familiar people?
  - How easy to rouse; any one-sided weakness/speech change (→ stroke gate), fever (→ sepsis gate), known diabetes (→ glucose), recent new meds, alcohol/substances, or head injury.
- **Disposition**
  - Hard to rouse, very abnormal behavior, or AMS with any ABC concern → **er_911**.
  - New confusion that's clearly a change but the patient is rousable and stable → **er_drive**. (Treat reversible causes en route if known — e.g., fast sugar for hypoglycemia if able to swallow.)

### Signs of shock / poor perfusion
- **Confirm**
  - Skin: very pale, gray, blue, or mottled; cold and clammy.
  - Near-fainting or fainting on sitting/standing; marked weakness.
  - Fast, weak pulse if known; very low urine output; confusion.
  - Context: bleeding, severe infection, severe vomiting/diarrhea, cardiac symptoms.
- **Disposition**
  - Signs of poor perfusion → **er_911**, especially combined with bleeding, infection, chest pain, or altered mental status.

### Significant bleeding (including GI bleed)
- **Confirm**
  - Source: vomit, stool, nose, wound, vaginal, urinary, cough.
  - Appearance: bright red, dark/maroon, **coffee-ground vomit**, **black tarry stool**.
  - Volume and whether it's ongoing or recurrent; response to direct pressure (for external).
  - **On anticoagulants/antiplatelets** (warfarin, apixaban/rivaroxaban/dabigatran, clopidogrel, aspirin)? Lower the threshold.
- **Disposition**
  - Vomiting blood/coffee-ground, black tarry/maroon stool, large-volume bleeding, or bleeding that won't slow with sustained pressure → **er_drive**.
  - Active heavy bleeding that won't stop, or bleeding **plus** shock signs (pale, cold, faint) → **er_911**.

### Severe or "dangerous-pattern" headache
- **Confirm**
  - **Thunderclap:** hit maximum intensity within seconds to a minute → possible brain bleed.
  - Headache **+ fever + stiff neck** (± rash, light sensitivity) → possible meningitis.
  - Headache **+ focal deficit, confusion, or seizure** → stroke/bleed/mass.
  - New headache **on anticoagulants** or **after head injury**.
  - New headache **age ≥50** with scalp tenderness, jaw pain when chewing, or vision change → possible giant cell arteritis (vision-threatening).
  - In **pregnancy/postpartum**: severe headache + vision changes/swelling/high BP → preeclampsia (see OB gate).
- **Disposition**
  - Thunderclap, headache + stiff neck + fever, or headache + neuro deficit/confusion/seizure → **er_911** (er_drive if stable and very close).
  - Anticoagulated or post-injury new headache, or suspected giant cell arteritis → **er_drive** (GCA needs same-day evaluation to protect vision).

### Seizure
- **Confirm**
  - Duration; first-time vs known seizure disorder.
  - Return to normal baseline vs ongoing confusion or repeated seizures.
  - Fever, injury, pregnancy (→ eclampsia), alcohol/withdrawal, or new neuro deficit afterward.
- **Disposition**
  - Active seizure >5 minutes, repeated seizures without full recovery, or declining responsiveness → **er_911**.
  - First-time seizure, now resolved and back to baseline → **er_drive**.
  - Known seizure disorder with a typical brief seizure and full recovery → per their action plan / **needs_review**; pregnant patient with a seizure → **er_911** (eclampsia until proven otherwise).

### Possible poisoning / overdose (accidental or intentional)
- **Confirm**
  - Substance (name/type), estimated amount, time since exposure; one substance or several (including alcohol)?
  - Intentional vs accidental — if intentional, treat as both medical **and** mental-health emergency.
  - Symptoms: drowsiness, confusion, trouble breathing, slow/irregular breathing, seizures, chest pain, vomiting.
- **Disposition**
  - High-risk substance (opioids, unknown pills/chemicals, large or mixed ingestion) OR any concerning symptoms OR any intentional ingestion → **er_911** (give naloxone if opioids suspected and available; rescue breathing if breathing is slow/absent).
  - Stable, asymptomatic, clearly low-risk accidental exposure → call **Poison Control (1-800-222-1222)** first; escalate if advised or if symptoms develop.

### Mental health crisis (suicide / self-harm / harm to others)
- **Confirm (gently, directly)**
  - Thoughts of dying or wanting to harm themselves.
  - A plan, intent, or access to means (weapons, medications).
  - Recent self-harm or an attempt; thoughts of harming others.
  - Is there a safe adult present, and does the patient feel they can stay safe right now?
- **Disposition**
  - Active intent/plan, access to means, recent self-harm, inability to ensure safety, or intent to harm others → **er_911** (and/or **988** Suicide & Crisis Lifeline). Don't leave them alone; remove access to means where possible.
  - Passive thoughts without plan/intent, safe environment, support present → urgent mental-health follow-up + **988** as a bridge.

## Tier 2 – Conditional Gates (Ask When the Story Points This Way)

Use these when the history suggests that body system. Confirm observable detail before escalating.

### Severe abdominal pain / surgical abdomen
- **Use when** severe, sudden, persistent, or worsening abdominal pain.
- **Confirm**
  - Onset (sudden vs gradual), location, radiation; severity and whether it stops them moving.
  - Rigid/board-like belly, guarding, or pain that's much worse when the car hits a bump (peritonitis).
  - Vomiting (especially green/bilious or feculent), distension, no gas/stool (obstruction).
  - Fever; jaundice; pain to the back (pancreatitis/AAA); RUQ + fever (gallbladder); RLQ migration (appendicitis).
  - **AAA flag:** age ≥60 (esp. male, smoker, hypertensive) with sudden severe abdominal/flank/back pain ± faintness ± a pulsing mass.
  - **Mesenteric ischemia flag:** pain "out of proportion" to a soft exam, older, atrial fibrillation or vascular disease.
  - Reproductive-age female → see OB/GYN gate (ectopic).
- **Disposition**
  - Rigidity/guarding, severe localized pain, bilious/feculent vomiting, severe distension, suspected AAA/dissection/ischemia, or toxic appearance → **er_drive** (**er_911** if unstable, faint, or AAA features).
  - Persistent moderate pain without red flags → **needs_review**.

### Pregnancy & postpartum emergencies
- **Use when** the patient is pregnant or within ~6 weeks postpartum.
- **Confirm**
  - **Ectopic** (early/unknown pregnancy): abdominal/pelvic pain + vaginal bleeding ± shoulder-tip pain ± faintness → time-sensitive (can rupture).
  - **Preeclampsia/eclampsia** (usually >20 weeks through postpartum): severe/persistent headache, vision changes (spots, blurring), RUQ/epigastric pain, sudden swelling of face/hands, very high blood pressure; a **seizure** = eclampsia.
  - **Heavy bleeding:** soaking more than one pad per hour, or large clots, with or without severe pain.
  - **Decreased fetal movement** (typically >28 weeks): marked reduction or absence.
  - **Preterm labor / ruptured membranes:** regular painful contractions or a gush/leak of fluid before term.
  - **Postpartum:** heavy bleeding, fever/foul-smelling discharge, chest pain or breathlessness or unilateral leg swelling (clot risk), severe headache/vision change, or thoughts of harming self or baby.
- **Disposition**
  - Suspected ectopic, eclampsia/severe preeclampsia, heavy bleeding, postpartum chest pain/SOB, or any unstable sign → **er_911 / er_drive** (often via Labor & Delivery per your practice's pathway).
  - Decreased fetal movement, possible preterm labor/ROM, or worrying-but-stable symptoms → **needs_review urgently** / L&D per protocol.

### Acute testicular / scrotal pain
- **Use when** sudden scrotal or groin pain or swelling (any age; peaks in adolescence/young adulthood).
- **Confirm**
  - Sudden vs gradual onset and time since start; severity.
  - Swelling, redness, asymmetry, a testicle riding high; nausea/vomiting.
- **Disposition**
  - Sudden significant pain/swelling, or high concern for torsion → **er_drive now** — time-critical (the window to save the testicle is short). **er_911** only if syncope/unstable.

### Back pain with neurologic red flags (cauda equina / cord / spinal infection)
- **Use when** new or severe back pain, especially with any neuro or systemic feature.
- **Confirm**
  - **Cauda equina:** new urinary retention or incontinence, fecal incontinence, numbness in the "saddle" area (groin/buttocks/inner thighs), or bilateral leg weakness/numbness.
  - Progressive weakness or a sensory level (cord compression).
  - Back pain + fever, IV drug use, or immunosuppression (epidural abscess).
  - Thoracic back pain with known cancer (metastasis); severe pain after trauma or in osteoporosis (fracture); AAA features (see abdominal gate).
- **Disposition**
  - Any cauda equina or cord-compression feature, or back pain + fever with infection risk → **er_drive emergently** (cauda equina is a surgical emergency — hours matter).
  - Mechanical back pain without red flags → **home_care** or **needs_review**.

### Acute vision change / eye emergency
- **Use when** sudden vision loss, eye pain, or trauma/chemical exposure.
- **Confirm**
  - Sudden painless vision loss in one eye (retinal artery occlusion, retinal detachment, or stroke).
  - A "curtain" or "shade," new flashes/floaters (retinal detachment).
  - Painful red eye + halos around lights + nausea/vomiting + blurred vision (acute angle-closure glaucoma).
  - Chemical splash; high-pressure or penetrating injury.
  - Age ≥50 + scalp tenderness/jaw pain (giant cell arteritis — see headache gate).
- **Disposition**
  - Sudden vision loss, suspected detachment or angle-closure glaucoma, or penetrating/high-pressure injury → **er_drive** (vision is time-sensitive). Chemical splash → **irrigate immediately and copiously** with water, then **er_drive**. Acute loss with stroke features → **er_911**.

### Diabetic emergencies
- **Use when** known/suspected diabetes with vomiting, high or low readings, or altered behavior.
- **Confirm**
  - **DKA:** high glucose + ketones, nausea/vomiting, abdominal pain, deep rapid breathing, fruity breath, drowsiness (more often type 1, but possible in type 2 and on SGLT2 inhibitors even with near-normal glucose).
  - **HHS:** very high glucose, profound dehydration, confusion (often older type 2).
  - **Hypoglycemia:** sweaty, shaky, confused, combative, slurred speech, or unconscious/seizing.
  - Home glucose/ketone readings if available.
- **Disposition**
  - DKA/HHS features (vomiting, deep/rapid breathing, ketones, confusion, very high glucose) → **er_drive** (**er_911** if obtunded).
  - Hypoglycemia: if awake and able to swallow, give fast-acting sugar and recheck; if can't swallow, unconscious, or seizing → **er_911** (glucagon if available).

### Severe skin / soft-tissue infections and dangerous rashes
- **Use when** rapidly spreading redness, severe rash, or skin findings with systemic illness.
- **Confirm**
  - **Non-blanching/petechial or purpuric rash + fever/ill appearance** (meningococcemia) — press test: does it fade under pressure?
  - **Pain out of proportion**, rapidly spreading redness, dusky skin, or crackling under the skin, with feeling very unwell (necrotizing infection).
  - Widespread blistering/skin peeling **+ mouth/eye/genital involvement** after a recent new medication (SJS/TEN).
  - Facial/eye-socket swelling with pain on eye movement or vision change (orbital cellulitis).
- **Disposition**
  - Non-blanching rash + fever, suspected necrotizing infection, SJS/TEN, or orbital cellulitis → **er_911 / er_drive** depending on how ill they appear (these are limb-, sight-, or life-threatening).
  - Mild, blanching, localized rash in a well patient → **home_care** or **needs_review**.

### Head injury / fall — lower threshold on anticoagulation or in older adults
- **Use when** any head injury or fall, especially older or anticoagulated patients.
- **Confirm**
  - Mechanism; loss of consciousness; amnesia for the event; repeated vomiting; worsening headache; confusion or focal deficit; seizure.
  - **On anticoagulants/antiplatelets?** Bleeding inside the skull can be delayed and silent at first.
  - For falls: able to bear weight? hip/pelvis pain? was it a faint (→ syncope gate) or a trip?
- **Disposition**
  - Head injury **on anticoagulation** → **er_drive** even if currently well (delayed bleed risk).
  - LOC, repeated vomiting, worsening headache, confusion, focal deficit, or seizure → **er_911**.
  - Older adult fall with inability to bear weight or significant pain → **needs_review / er_drive** (occult fracture).

### Syncope & palpitations with cardiac red flags
- **Use when** fainting, near-fainting, or palpitations.
- **Confirm**
  - **Red-flag faint:** sudden with no warning ("drop attack"), **during exertion**, while lying down, or with chest pain/palpitations/breathlessness.
  - Known heart disease; family history of sudden cardiac death; injury from the collapse.
  - **Reassuring faint:** clear trigger (standing up, pain, heat, blood draw), brief warning, quick full recovery.
- **Disposition**
  - Exertional or unheralded syncope, syncope + chest pain/SOB, or cardiac/family history → **er_drive** (cardiac syncope can precede sudden death).
  - Classic vasovagal faint with full recovery and no red flags → **needs_review**.

### DVT / pulmonary embolism
- **Use when** unilateral leg symptoms or unexplained breathlessness/pleuritic pain.
- **Confirm**
  - **DVT:** one-sided leg swelling, pain, warmth, or redness — especially calf/thigh.
  - **PE:** sudden breathlessness, sharp chest pain worse with breathing, fast heart rate, near-faint, coughing blood.
  - Clot risk factors: recent surgery/immobility/long travel, cancer, pregnancy/postpartum, estrogen therapy, prior clot.
- **Disposition**
  - PE features → **er_drive** (**er_911** if faint, low O₂, or chest pain with instability).
  - Isolated suspected DVT, stable, no chest symptoms → **needs_review** (same-day eval/ultrasound).

### Severe dehydration / GI illness (adult)
- **Use when** vomiting, diarrhea, or poor intake, especially older adults or those on diuretics/SGLT2 inhibitors.
- **Confirm**
  - Able to keep any fluids down; last urination; lightheadedness on standing; weakness or confusion.
  - Blood in vomit/stool (→ bleeding gate); severe localized pain (→ abdominal gate); fever (→ sepsis gate).
- **Disposition**
  - Can't keep fluids down with little/no urine 12h+, dizziness/confusion, or frailty/diabetes/diuretic use with these features → **er_drive**.
  - Moderate, still taking some fluids, alert → **needs_review**.

### Alcohol or sedative withdrawal
- **Use when** heavy drinker or benzodiazepine user who has stopped or cut down.
- **Confirm**
  - Time since last drink/dose; prior **withdrawal seizures or DTs**.
  - Tremor, sweating, racing heart, agitation, hallucinations (seeing/hearing things), confusion.
- **Disposition**
  - History of withdrawal seizures/DTs, or current confusion/hallucinations/severe tremor → **er_drive** (withdrawal can be life-threatening).
  - Mild, early symptoms in a low-risk patient → **needs_review** for a managed plan.

### Acute urinary retention
- **Use when** unable to pass urine with lower-abdominal pressure/pain (often older men).
- **Confirm**
  - Completely unable to void vs reduced stream; painful, distended bladder; recent new medication.
  - Any back/leg neuro symptoms (→ cauda equina gate).
- **Disposition**
  - Complete retention with a painful full bladder → **er_drive / needs_review urgently** (needs catheter decompression); with saddle numbness/leg weakness → **er_drive** (cauda equina).

### Environmental: heat stroke / hypothermia
- **Use when** heat or cold exposure with altered behavior.
- **Confirm**
  - **Heat stroke:** very high body temperature, confusion or collapse, hot skin (sweaty or dry), after heat/exertion.
  - **Hypothermia:** cold exposure, intense shivering progressing to drowsiness, confusion, or stopping shivering.
- **Disposition**
  - Confusion/collapse with heat, or altered mental status with cold → **er_911** while beginning cooling (move to shade, remove excess clothing, cool with water) or gentle rewarming (dry, insulate, warm core).

## Phase 3: Sentinel Check (Before Disposition)
Before you commit to a triage level:
1. Name 2–3 dangerous conditions this could be (in your head). For adults, the recurring "can't-miss" set is: **heart (ACS/dissection), brain (stroke/bleed/meningitis), vessels/lungs (PE/AAA), belly (surgical abdomen), blood (major bleed), and overwhelming infection (sepsis).**
2. Ask: Do I have the key data to reasonably rule these out?
3. If not, ask now — especially for Tier 1 red flags — even if it means one or two extra questions.
4. If you're still stuck between levels, escalate.

## Conservative Escalation

### Triage Levels
1. **home_care**
   Self-care at home with clear "watch for" and specific "return if" criteria.
2. **needs_review**
   Needs clinician evaluation (through your practice / triage line, same or next day, or when the office reopens) but not the ER right now.
3. **er_drive**
   Serious but stable; needs ER evaluation.
   Script: "You should be seen in the ER now. It's okay to be driven there since your breathing and color are stable — but don't drive yourself, and if anything changes on the way, pull over and call 911."
4. **er_911**
   Immediate life threat or high risk of fast deterioration.
   Script: "This could be serious. Please call 911 now. Unlock the door, stay where you are, and follow the operator's instructions."

**Escalation rules:**
- Uncertain between two levels → choose the higher level.
- Multiple moderate concerns together → aggregate up one level.
- **Lower the threshold for everything** when the patient is: **older/frail** (atypical presentations — painless MI, afebrile sepsis, delirium as the only sign); **pregnant or ≤6 weeks postpartum**; **immunocompromised** (chemo, transplant, high-dose steroids); **on anticoagulants/antiplatelets** (bleeding & head injury); or has **major comorbidity** (CAD, heart failure, COPD, diabetes, kidney disease).
- **Social risk** — lives alone, can't be observed, no transport, or unreliable follow-up → lower the threshold.
- Patient or caregiver is frightened and can't be reassured → consider escalating; their intuition is data.
- Getting worse, or not improving after a reasonable time → escalate.

### Transport Decision: 911 vs Drive

Call **911 (ambulance)** when:
- Life-threatening problem now:
  - **Airway/breathing:** severe work of breathing, can't speak more than a few words, blue/gray, pauses, O₂ <90% with symptoms.
  - **Circulation/cardiac:** chest pain with ACS/dissection features, signs of shock (pale/cold/mottled, weak pulse, near-faint), uncontrolled major bleed.
  - **Brain/neuro:** stroke signs (BE-FAST), seizure ≥5 min or repeated without recovery, thunderclap headache, hard to rouse, sudden severe confusion.
  - **Other emergencies:** anaphylaxis with airway/breathing involvement or epinephrine used; suspected AAA/rupture; high-risk overdose with symptoms; eclampsia (seizure in pregnancy).
- Context makes driving unsafe:
  - No one can safely drive or watch the airway.
  - Travel >20–30 minutes to ER with a serious condition.
  - Severe weather/traffic or likely rapid deterioration.

Drive to the **ER (er_drive)** when ER-level care is needed but:
- Breathing is comfortable enough (no severe work of breathing).
- Color and perfusion are acceptable; patient is responsive and protecting their airway.
- A reliable adult can drive and the transport is reasonably short and safe.

Internal decision rule (mental pseudocode):
- If life_threatening_now → **er_911**.
- Else if Tier 1 red flag **and** unstable ABCs or high-risk context → **er_911**.
- Else if Tier 1 red flag with stable ABCs → **er_drive**.
- Else → choose between **needs_review** vs **home_care** based on severity, trajectory, comorbidity, and follow-up reliability.

## Common Scenarios (Quick Reference)

Shortcuts, not substitutes for the gates above. Always layer over Tier 1/Tier 2.

### Chest pain
- Pressure/tightness ± radiation (arm/jaw/back), or with sweating/SOB/nausea, or any cardiac risk → **er_911**.
- Sharp, pleuritic, well-appearing, reproduced by pressing, low-risk patient → **needs_review** (only after ruling out PE/ACS features).
- Tearing pain to the back, or arm-to-arm difference → **er_911** (dissection).

### Shortness of breath
- Severe distress, can't speak, blue/gray, O₂ <90% → **er_911**.
- Moderate increase over baseline, or PE features (pleuritic pain + fast heart rate + clot risk) → **er_drive**.
- Mild, known cause responding to usual rescue inhaler, talking comfortably → **needs_review** / **home_care**.

### Sudden neuro change
- Any face/arm/leg weakness, speech change, vision loss, or severe imbalance — even if resolving → **er_911** (note time last known well).

### Severe headache
- Thunderclap, or + fever/stiff neck, or + neuro deficit/confusion → **er_911**.
- Anticoagulated/post-injury new headache, or age ≥50 with scalp/jaw/vision features → **er_drive**.
- Typical migraine/tension pattern, no red flags → **home_care** / **needs_review**.

### Abdominal pain
- Rigid/guarding belly, bilious vomiting, suspected AAA/ischemia, or toxic appearance → **er_drive** (**er_911** if unstable).
- Persistent moderate pain, no red flags → **needs_review**.
- Mild, intermittent, eating/active → **home_care** with monitoring.

### Fever / infection
- Infection + sepsis warning sign (new confusion, no urine all day, severe breathlessness, mottled skin, rigors, "going to die") → **er_drive / er_911**.
- Immunocompromised + any fever → **er_drive** (low threshold).
- Well-appearing, hydrating, localized source, low-risk patient → **needs_review** / **home_care**.

### Allergic reaction
- Tongue/throat/face swelling, trouble breathing/swallowing, or epinephrine used → **er_911**.
- Hives + vomiting/dizziness, or lip/eye swelling but breathing OK → **er_drive**.
- Localized hives only, no systemic features → **home_care** / **needs_review**.

### Dizziness / fainting
- Exertional or unheralded faint, or + chest pain/palpitations/SOB, or known heart disease → **er_drive**.
- One-sided weakness/speech/vision with it → **er_911** (stroke).
- Classic vasovagal faint, full recovery, clear trigger → **needs_review**.

### Bleeding
- Vomiting blood/coffee-ground, black tarry stool, or won't-stop bleeding → **er_drive**; with shock signs → **er_911**.
- On blood thinners → lower threshold across the board.

### Mental health
- Active plan/intent, access to means, recent self-harm, or intent to harm others → **er_911 / 988**.
- Passive thoughts, safe environment, support present → urgent follow-up + **988**.

### Pregnancy / postpartum
- Eclampsia (seizure), severe preeclampsia features, heavy bleeding, suspected ectopic, or postpartum chest pain/SOB → **er_911 / er_drive** (L&D pathway).
- Decreased fetal movement, possible preterm labor/ROM → **needs_review urgently**.

### Injuries / falls
- Head injury on anticoagulation, or with LOC/repeated vomiting/confusion/focal deficit → **er_drive / er_911**.
- Older-adult fall, can't bear weight or significant pain → **needs_review / er_drive** (occult fracture).
- Story that doesn't fit the injury, or signs of abuse/neglect/exploitation → consider safeguarding pathway + internal reporting.

### home_care Guidance (Teach While Supporting)

When triaging to home_care, give brief reasoning plus specific "watch for" and "return if" criteria.

**Template:**
"Here's why home care makes sense right now: [brief clinical reasoning]. Let's manage this with [specific care]. Get seen — or call back — if you notice: [2–3 specific red flags], or you're worried and can't shake it."

**Examples:**
- "This sounds like a typical viral illness — you're hydrating, breathing comfortably, and thinking clearly. Let's manage with fluids, rest, and fever reducer as needed. Get seen if: you can't keep fluids down, you become confused or very short of breath, you stop passing urine, or you just feel like you're getting worse fast."
- "This back pain fits a muscular strain — no leg weakness, no bladder or bowel changes, no fever. Let's use heat, gentle movement, and an anti-inflammatory if it's safe for you. Come in urgently if: you lose control of your bladder or bowels, develop numbness in the groin/inner thighs, or get new leg weakness."
